Sex in the Digital Age

The modern digital environment has injected the presence of and ease of accessibility to depictions of sexual acts and intimations preventing a mutually established context amongst persons, impacting sexual and social norms. This impact often crosses many social aspects as the majority of those access the internet on mobile devices (comscore, 2017). This shared utility between an individual’s communication and access to sexual material is different from times where collective access to sexual material in American history occurred in adult theatres popular in the 1970s, or peepshows, which were prevalent prior to the 70s. This newly individualized connection to sexual material which grew out of the Internet revolution arrived closely after the sexual revolution that occurred in the US in the 1960s through the 1980s. While beforehand, sexual material was present for independent consumption with erotic publications it has never been as subjective to interests of the individual.

While the genesis of sex in the digital age may have liberated individuality it has also caused a rise in egocentric sexuality with many choosing their own version of sexual expression using pornography. This choice of exercising one’s sexual liberation is seemingly apparent in several societal facets. Divorce rates doubles amongst couples where porn is watched by one of the partners (American Sociological Association, 2016). Also, physical and sexual abuse has decreased by 56% and 62% respectively from 1992 to 2010 (Crimes Against Children Center, 2012). While it is difficult to correlate this decrease in abuse directly to the emergence of internet pornography both of these statistics illustrate a prominence of individualistic attitudes and expressions of sexuality over that of conventional sexual relationships. This independent understanding of sex and internalized sexual interests not only affects our decisions within relationships but also our communication.

Porn's Internet Bandwith: 30 percent of all data transferred across the Internet is porn.

The shared utility of access to erotic material and communication through mobile devices, such as smartphones or tablets, creates an unprecedented equivalence between social and sexual behavior. According to Pornhub, a popular pornographic website in the US, roughly half of viewers of the site accessed it using mobile devices in 2013. While it is up to the user to discern socially acceptable behavior, the separation of sex from a social activity such as masturbating to pornographic material, permits an environment of objective sexual expression versus social sexual expression. Further inhibiting concern or empathy for another’s sexual views. While the discernment between fantasy and reality in pornographic material is reasonable for adults that encounter explicit material, one casualty of digital age sex may be adolescents. 90 percent of boys and 60% of girls by the age of 18 years old have have seen porn (CovenantEyes, 2017), with the average age of initial exposure to porn being 11 years old (Internet Filter Review, 2006). This proliferation amongst young adolescents is troubling as the important developmental stage of 12-14 years old is when concern with body image, confidence, and social pressures are increased with begin of puberty and increased hormonal changes (CDC, 2017). Regardless of age however, the role of pornography has made lasting effects on several age groups and is clear in our internet habits and vernacular. This change has helped grow propagate the modern parallelism of sexual and emotional relationships.

25% of all internet searches are pornography related. Totaling roughly 68 million searches a day.

There is one substantial difference between internet users’ visits between porn-sites versus non porn-sites; duration. Comparing similar video streaming sites YouTube and Pornhub, the average session duration is 40 minutes and 9.5 minutes respectively according to Google and Pornhub statistics. This shows a greater investment in time for non-erotic video streaming and furthers the theory of porn’s objectification of sex and removal of the social aspects from the act of sex. In a study of nearly 1,000 high school students 28% reported sending a naked picture of themselves through text or email (Arch Pediatr Adolesc Med, 2012). Additionally, the students that ‘sexted’, sexual messaging via mobile device, were more likely to engage in sexual activity than those students that did not sext. Meaning that the sharing of intimations via mobile device messaging is a new step in familiarity amongst many young adults that participate in sexual intercourse. Creating a shared view of the objectivity of sex amongst both partners and precipitating an implied acceptance of a sexual relationship parallel to that of an emotional relationship.
